## Remittances Rise 17% in March

### Key Takeaways
* Remittances in March were 17% higher than in February, reaching $3.8 billion.
* The influx was the highest this fiscal year, despite being 5% lower than March FY25.
* The ongoing Middle East conflict did not significantly impact remittances from the region.
* Power consumers will face an additional burden of Rs10.57 billion due to a fuel cost adjustment increase.

## Remittances Defy Middle East Turbulence
The State Bank of Pakistan (SBP) announced that remittances in March saw a 17% increase compared to February, defying expectations that the ongoing Middle East conflict would negatively impact these inflows. The $3.8 billion received in March was the highest this fiscal year, although it was 5% lower than the $4 billion received in March of the previous fiscal year. This resilience in remittances is a significant positive for the Pakistan economy today, as these inflows are crucial for stabilizing the country's foreign exchange reserves and supporting the PKR exchange rate.

The fact that remittances from the Middle East did not decline significantly despite the conflict is particularly noteworthy. It suggests that Pakistani workers in the region are continuing to send money back home, which is vital for the livelihoods of many families in Pakistan. The SBP's announcement is a welcome piece of news for the Pakistan economy, which has been facing challenges on multiple fronts, including high inflation and a large trade deficit.

## Power Tariff Hikes and Fuel Adjustments
In other news, the National Electric Power Regulatory Authority (Nepra) notified an increase in the fuel cost adjustment (FCA) of Rs1.42 per unit, which will result in an additional burden of Rs10.57 billion on power consumers. This move is expected to have a mixed impact on the economy, as it will increase the cost of production for industries and households, but will also help power generation companies to recover their costs.

The Federation of Pakistan Chambers of Commerce and Industry (FPCCI) has warned that repeated hikes in power tariffs threaten the viability of industries, which could lead to higher unemployment and lower economic growth. On the other hand, the increase in FCA will help to reduce the circular debt in the power sector, which has been a major challenge for the government.

## Global Markets React to US-Iran Ceasefire
The dollar fell to its lowest level in a month against a basket of major currencies after the US and Iran agreed to a two-week ceasefire. The euro rose 0.88% to $1.1696, while the Canadian dollar strengthened to a near two-week high against its US counterpart. The ceasefire agreement has injected optimism into markets, boosting risk assets and leading to a decline in the price of oil.

The impact of the US-Iran ceasefire on the Pakistan economy will depend on various factors, including the duration of the ceasefire and its impact on global oil prices. A prolonged ceasefire could lead to lower oil prices, which would be beneficial for Pakistan, as the country is a net importer of oil. On the other hand, a breakdown in the ceasefire could lead to higher oil prices, which would increase Pakistan's import bill and put pressure on the country's foreign exchange reserves.

## Power Tariffs May Rise Further
In related news, power tariffs may rise by over Rs2 per unit due to an increase in furnace oil prices. The use of furnace oil in power generation increased in March, driven by a decline in hydropower generation and reduced supply of re-gasified liquefied natural gas (RLNG). The increase in furnace oil prices will lead to a higher fuel cost adjustment, which will be passed on to consumers.

The proposed increase in power tariffs will have a significant impact on the Pakistan economy, as it will increase the cost of production for industries and households. The government will need to balance the need to reduce the circular debt in the power sector with the need to protect consumers from high electricity prices.
